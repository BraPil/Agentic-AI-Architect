NASA figured out how to write bulletproof code in 2006 and almost nobody used it.

Gerard Holzmann at JPL published the "Power of Ten," ten rules for safety-critical software. Simple stuff: every function fits on one printed page, every loop has a fixed upper bound, no dynamic memory after initialization, minimum two assertions per function. He kept it to ten rules because he'd watched organizations write 100+ page coding standards that developers just ignored.

The problem was always enforcement. You could put these rules on a wiki and tell people to follow them, but verifying compliance across hundreds of thousands of lines of code required either expensive tooling or a very patient human reviewer. Neither scaled well outside of JPL.

I think about this a lot, actually. Not just the Power of Ten specifically, but this whole category of things: ideas that were clearly right but too expensive to enforce. Checklists that worked in theory but fell apart in practice. The gap between knowing what good looks like and actually doing it consistently.

That gap is what AI agents collapse. You encode the rules, the agent applies them on every commit, every PR, every deployment. It doesn't have an opinion about whether the rules are worth following. It doesn't skip the check because it's been a long week.

I'm building agent workflows at Whimsey Labs right now, and honestly the hardest part isn't getting agents to follow rules. It's figuring out which rules are actually worth encoding. That's where the Power of Ten is helpful as a reference. Holzmann spent years figuring out which ten rules would give you the most coverage with the least friction. That kind of thinking transfers directly.

Still figuring out the velocity tradeoff. But the distance between "best practices exist" and "best practices get enforced" just shrank by a lot.
