😅 Tool-calling in AI agents is annoyingly brittle for large enterprise use-cases. This is a nice paper that addresses the issue at the model level.

Today you have two options: prompt engineering (easy to deploy but breaks when anything shifts) or fine-tuning (more reliable but you pay the cost of training, maintenance, and the model might forget other stuff).

The researchers dug into what's actually happening inside the model when it fails to call a tool it should call. They found something interesting: if you look at the mid-layer activations, you can almost perfectly predict when the model should use a tool. The signal is there. The model internally "knows" a tool is needed.

But it doesn't act on it. It stays conservative and just doesn't enter tool mode. They call this the "Lazy Agent" failure mode - a gap between what the model knows and what it does.

Instead of retraining, they intervene at inference time. They built ASA (Activation Steering Adapter), a small controller that watches the mid-layer activations and nudges the model when the internal signals say "use a tool" but the model isn't following through.

A few highlights from the paper:
- The fix is training-free and requires no weight updates
- Only ~20KB of portable assets you plug in at inference
